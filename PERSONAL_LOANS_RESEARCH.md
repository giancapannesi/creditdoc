## 1. HOW TOP SITES HANDLE PERSONAL LOAN SEARCH/FILTERING

### NerdWallet (nerdwallet.com/personal-loans)

**Model:** Hybrid -- editorial comparison site + licensed marketplace (NerdWallet Compare, Inc., NMLS #1617539)

**Filters offered:**
- Loan amount (slider/input)
- Credit score range (dropdown: Excellent 720+, Good 690-719, Fair 630-689, Bad 300-629)
- State of residence (dropdown)
- Loan purpose (debt consolidation, home improvement, major purchase, etc.)

**Rating system:**
- Star ratings on a 5-star scale
- Weighted average across 5 categories:
  - Affordability (25%) -- APR, origination fees, rate discounts
  - Customer Experience (20%) -- service accessibility, payment flexibility, mobile app
  - Underwriting and Eligibility (20%) -- credit profile acceptance, hard check requirements
  - Loan Flexibility (20%) -- loan types, amounts, terms, direct creditor payment
  - Application Process (15%) -- pre-qualification, online info, funding speed
- Surveys 30+ lenders annually; continuous updates for rate changes
- Ratings may be lowered for regulatory actions in past 3 years
- Star ratings are NOT influenced by compensation (per NerdWallet's disclosure)

**APR display:**
- Shows APR ranges per lender (e.g., "8.99% - 25.49% APR")
- Includes disclaimer: "Rates controlled by third party advertisers and subject to change without notice"
- States that lowest rates only available to most qualified borrowers
- Notes other fees may apply (origination, late payment)

**Personal financial info collected:**
- For pre-qualification: yes -- collects income, employment, desired amount, credit score range
- For editorial comparison: no -- just filters by credit score range and state

**Broker vs. comparison distinction:**
- NerdWallet Compare, Inc. holds actual broker licenses in multiple states (Connecticut, Kansas, Louisiana, Mississippi, New Hampshire, New Jersey, Virginia)
- California Finance Lender License 60DBO-74812
- Texas Regulated Lender License #160370
- Explicit disclaimer: "NerdWallet Compare, Inc. is not acting as an agent and does not endorse or recommend any of the products of any lender"
- "Will not make any mortgage loan commitments or fund any mortgage loans"

**Key disclaimers on loan pages:**
- "Advertiser Disclosure" banner at top of every comparison page
- "NerdWallet's partners compensate them for advertisements"
- "Partner compensation is one of several factors that may affect which products are highlighted and where they appear"
- "These factors do not influence editors' opinions or ratings"
- APR range disclaimer with "subject to change" language

### LendingTree (lendingtree.com/personal)

**Model:** Licensed marketplace/lead generator (NOT editorial-only)

**How it works:**
- User fills out detailed form (loan amount, purpose, employment, income, housing)
- LendingTree performs soft credit pull
- Returns up to 20 personalized loan offers from partner lenders
- Lenders then contact the consumer directly to compete for business

**Filters/search:**
- Not traditional filters -- it is a form-driven process
- Inputs: loan amount, loan purpose, credit rating (self-reported)
- Results can be sorted by APR or monthly payment

**Rating system:**
- Editorial reviews with star ratings
- Also shows customer reviews from verified borrowers

**APR display:**
- Shows personalized pre-qualified APR rates after form submission
- General rate tables on editorial pages

**Personal financial info:**
- Collects extensive personal data: income, employment, housing status, SSN (for soft pull)
- This is what makes them a marketplace, not just an editorial site

**Broker vs. comparison distinction:**
- LendingTree LLC is explicitly a "MARKETING LEAD GENERATOR AND IS A DULY LICENSED MORTGAGE BROKER"
- Holds NMLS registration and state licenses
- This is the key difference: they collect personal data and perform credit pulls

**Key disclaimers:**
- Prominent "how we make money" explanation
- Disclosure that compensation may affect listing order
- Disclaimer that not all consumers will qualify for advertised rates

### Bankrate (bankrate.com/loans/personal-loans)

- Credit score categories (excellent, good, fair, bad)

  - Affordability -- rates, penalties, fees

- Explicit disclosure: "Although Bankrate receives compensation from some lenders, partnerships do not influence decisions"

**APR display:**

**Personal financial info:**

**Broker vs. comparison distinction:**
- Bankrate is an "independent, advertising-supported publisher and comparison service"

**Key disclaimers:**

## 2. LEGAL/REGULATORY LANDSCAPE

### 2.1 Comparison Site vs. Broker vs. Marketplace -- Legal Definitions

**Editorial Comparison/Directory Site (Safest):**
- Publishes reviews, ratings, and comparisons of financial products
- Shows publicly available rate ranges from lender websites
- Earns revenue from affiliate links (click-through, not lead submission)
- Does NOT collect personal financial information
- Does NOT perform credit checks
- Does NOT negotiate terms or arrange loans
- Does NOT pass user data to lenders
- Example: Bankrate's editorial pages

**Lead Generator (Middle Ground -- License May Be Required):**
- Collects consumer information (name, email, phone, desired loan amount)
- Passes leads to partner lenders
- Does NOT perform credit checks or negotiate terms
- License requirements VARY BY STATE
- CFPB and state regulators increasingly scrutinizing this model

**Marketplace/Broker (Most Regulated):**
- Collects detailed personal/financial info
- Performs soft credit pulls
- Shows personalized pre-qualified rates
- Arranges connections between borrowers and lenders
- REQUIRES state-by-state broker licensing and NMLS registration
- Examples: LendingTree, Credible, NerdWallet Compare

### 2.2 TILA (Truth in Lending Act) / Regulation Z Requirements

**Who it applies to:**
All persons, including non-creditors, must comply with advertising provisions of Regulation Z. This means even a comparison site that advertises credit products must follow these rules.

**Trigger Terms for Closed-End Credit (Personal Loans):**

If ANY of these "trigger terms" appear in advertising, ADDITIONAL disclosures are MANDATORY:

| Trigger Term | Example | Required Additional Disclosures |
|-------------|---------|-------------------------------|
| Down payment amount | "Only $100 down" | APR, repayment terms, whether APR may increase |
| Number of payments / repayment period | "60 monthly payments" or "5-year term" | APR, repayment terms, whether APR may increase |
| Amount of any payment | "Payments as low as $150/mo" | APR, repayment terms, whether APR may increase |
| Amount of any finance charge | "$5/month carrying charge" | APR, repayment terms, whether APR may increase |

**What is NOT a trigger term:**
- APR alone (showing "8.99% - 25.49% APR" does NOT trigger additional disclosures)
- Vague payment references ("monthly payment to suit your needs")
- Vague time references ("take years to repay")
- "No down payment" (negative trigger does NOT apply to closed-end credit)

**CRITICAL FINDING FOR CREDITDOC:**
Showing APR ranges (e.g., "6.99% - 35.99% APR") is SAFE and does NOT trigger Regulation Z additional disclosure requirements. This is how Bankrate and NerdWallet display rates on editorial pages. However, the moment you show specific monthly payment amounts (e.g., "$150/month for 36 months"), you trigger mandatory disclosures.

### 2.3 State Licensing Requirements

**States with notable consumer loan broker/lender licensing requirements:**

- **California (DFPI):** California Financing Law requires licensing for anyone engaging in the business of a "finance lender" or "finance broker." Brokering = negotiating or performing any act as broker in connection with loans. Referral fees and lead generation can trigger licensing. Penalties: void contracts, fines, criminal liability.

- **New York (DFS):** Strict licensing requirements. Operating without a license can render contracts unenforceable.

- **Other strict states:** Nevada, Arizona, Washington, Connecticut, New Jersey, Virginia, Georgia, Massachusetts

**Key Question: Does an editorial comparison site need a license?**

The answer depends on WHAT the site does:
- Publishing rate ranges from publicly available lender data: **NO license needed**
- Collecting user info and passing it to lenders as leads: **License MAY be required (state-dependent)**
- Performing credit checks and showing personalized rates: **License REQUIRED in most states**
- Negotiating terms or acting as intermediary: **License REQUIRED everywhere**

**NMLS Registration:**
- NMLS (Nationwide Multistate Licensing System) is required for mortgage brokers/lenders
- For personal loan comparison sites, NMLS registration is NOT required unless the site acts as a broker or lead generator that "solicits" or "assists" borrowers
- Pure editorial/directory sites do NOT need NMLS registration

### 2.4 FTC Requirements

**Endorsement Guides (16 CFR Part 255):**
- Material connections (affiliate compensation) MUST be disclosed "clearly and conspicuously"
- Disclosure must be near affiliate links, not buried in footer
- Rankings influenced by compensation MUST disclose that fact
- If rankings are claimed to be "objective" or "unbiased" but are actually pay-for-play, this is DECEPTIVE under FTC Act
- Penalty: up to $50,120 per violation (2024 adjusted)

**LendEDU Enforcement (2020):**
- FTC fined comparison shopping site $350,000 for:
  - Falsely claiming rankings were "objective, accurate, and unbiased"
  - Rankings were actually sold to highest bidders
  - 90% of Trustpilot reviews were fake (written by employees/family)
- Lesson: NEVER claim objectivity if compensation influences rankings

**Credit Karma Enforcement (2022):**
- FTC fined $3 million for deceptive "pre-approved" claims
- Users told they had "90% odds" but nearly 1/3 were rejected
- Lesson: NEVER use "pre-approved" or "pre-qualified" language unless it is accurate

### 2.5 CFPB Guidance (Circular 2024-01)

**CFPB Circular on Digital Comparison-Shopping Tools (Feb 29, 2024):**

The CFPB warned that comparison-shopping tools can violate the Consumer Financial Protection Act by:

1. **Steering** -- directing consumers to products based on compensation to the operator, not consumer benefit
2. **Preferencing** -- giving prominent placement to higher-paying partners
3. **Dynamic bidding** -- allowing lenders to bid for top placement
4. **Self-preferencing** -- favoring operator's own products/affiliates
5. **Misleading surveys/filters** -- creating impression of objective evaluation when results are compensation-driven
6. **Warm handoffs** -- routing consumers to lenders based on compensation rather than fit

**Prohibited practices include:**
- Labeling a product as "featured" or "recommended" because it pays more
- Rotating lenders into top positions based on payment
- Steering consumers via chatbot or filter that appears objective but is pay-to-play
- Volume allocation practices that manipulate which lender gets leads

**HOWEVER -- CFPB Status as of March 2026:**
- Trump administration has gutted the CFPB: ~88% workforce reduction, 90% of supervision division cut
- 16 enforcement actions dismissed with prejudice
- ~70 interpretive rules, circulars, and advisory opinions rescinded
- The CFPB Circular 2024-01 may have been among those rescinded
- Current CFPB enforcement risk: LOW
- But FTC enforcement and state AG enforcement: STILL ACTIVE
- States (especially California, New York, Illinois) are expected to increase their own enforcement

### 2.6 Can CreditDoc Show "Pre-Qualified" Rates?

**Without being a broker? NO.**

Pre-qualification requires:
- Collecting personal financial data (income, employment, SSN)
- Performing a soft credit pull
- Showing personalized rates based on that data

Every major site that offers pre-qualification (NerdWallet, LendingTree, Credible, Credit Karma) holds broker/marketplace licenses.

**CreditDoc should NOT offer pre-qualification** unless it obtains appropriate state licenses (estimated cost: $25,000-$100,000+ for multi-state licensing, plus ongoing compliance costs).

### 2.7 Lead Generation -- Does Passing User Info to Lenders Make CreditDoc a Broker?

**Possibly yes, depending on:**
- What data is collected (name/email only vs. financial details)
- How data is used (general marketing vs. loan-matching)
- Whether CreditDoc receives compensation tied to loan outcomes
- State-specific definitions of "brokering" and "soliciting"

**Activities that can trigger broker classification:**
- Transmitting confidential borrower data to lenders with expectation of compensation (California)
- Participating in loan negotiations
- Preparing loan documents
- Counseling or advising borrowers on specific loan products
- Providing the identity of potential borrowers (Arizona)

**Activities that are generally SAFE:**
- Publishing editorial reviews and comparisons
- Showing publicly available rate ranges
- Using standard affiliate links (user clicks through to lender's own site)
- Not collecting financial data beyond what's needed for basic filtering (credit score range, loan amount range)

### SAFEST Approach for CreditDoc

**Recommended filters (editorial model):**
1. **Loan amount needed** -- Dropdown ranges: $1K-$5K, $5K-$10K, $10K-$25K, $25K-$50K, $50K+
2. **Credit score range** -- Dropdown: Excellent (720+), Good (670-719), Fair (580-669), Poor (300-579)
3. **Loan purpose** -- Dropdown: Debt consolidation, Home improvement, Major purchase, Medical, Auto, Emergency, Other
4. **State** -- Dropdown (to filter by lender availability)

**Filters to AVOID:**
- Specific monthly payment calculator (triggers Reg Z)
- Income input (triggers lead gen classification)
- SSN or date of birth (absolutely not for editorial site)
- "Check my rate" or "Get personalized rates" (implies brokering)

### Why This Works

Filtering by loan amount range and credit score range does NOT make CreditDoc a broker because:
- These are self-reported ranges, not verified data
- No credit pull is performed
- CreditDoc is filtering its editorial database, not matching users with lender offers
- The user is choosing which lenders to explore, not being "matched" by CreditDoc
- This is identical to how Bankrate operates (no broker license needed)

## 4. THE "RATING" QUESTION -- HOW TO RATE LENDERS SAFELY

### How the Major Sites Rate Lenders

**NerdWallet:** 5-star editorial rating, weighted across 5 categories (27 subcategories), annual survey of 30+ lenders. Explicitly states ratings are independent of compensation.

**Bankrate:** 5-star score from 20-point internal scale, weighted across 4 categories. Strict editorial firewall. Published methodology page.

**SuperMoney:** User/community reviews from verified members. No proprietary editorial score.

### Can CreditDoc Assign Star Ratings? YES -- But With Safeguards

**What you MUST do:**

1. **Publish a transparent methodology page** -- Explain exactly how ratings are calculated, what categories are weighted, and what data sources are used. Both NerdWallet and Bankrate do this. CreditDoc already has `/src/pages/methodology.astro`.

2. **Ensure ratings are genuinely independent of compensation** -- Ratings CANNOT be influenced by which lenders pay affiliate commissions. This was the core issue in the LendEDU FTC action.

3. **Document your rating process** -- Keep records of how each rating was determined. If the FTC ever investigates, you need to prove your ratings are legitimate.

4. **Use clear language** -- "CreditDoc Rating" or "Editorial Score" rather than implying user consensus.

5. **Add regulatory action check** -- Like NerdWallet, lower ratings for lenders with recent CFPB/FTC/state enforcement actions.

### Rating Categories Recommended for CreditDoc Personal Loans

Based on industry standards:

| Category | Weight | What It Measures |
|----------|--------|-----------------|
| Affordability | 30% | APR range, origination fees, prepayment penalties, rate discounts |
| Eligibility & Access | 25% | Min credit score, income requirements, state availability, funding speed |
| Transparency | 20% | How clearly rates/fees/terms are published, no hidden charges |
| Customer Experience | 15% | Support hours, online portal, mobile app, complaint resolution |
| Loan Flexibility | 10% | Term options, amount range, payment flexibility, hardship options |

### Defamation Risk

**Low risk if:**
- Ratings are based on verifiable facts (published rates, fees, terms)
- Methodology is transparent and consistently applied
- You allow lenders to dispute inaccuracies
- You avoid making false statements of fact

**Higher risk if:**
- You make subjective negative claims without factual basis
- You single out a lender for unusually harsh treatment
- Your rating seems designed to punish a lender that doesn't pay affiliate commissions

**BBB Ratings:**
- Most major personal loan lenders DO have BBB profiles
- BBB ratings (A+ through F) are meaningful trust signals for consumers
- CreditDoc can reference BBB ratings as one data point (like the existing credit repair lender schema does)
- DO NOT present BBB ratings as CreditDoc's own rating

## 5. WHAT CREDITDOC SHOULD NOT DO

### Features That Cross the Line from "Directory" to "Broker/Marketplace"

| Feature | Risk Level | Why |
|---------|-----------|-----|
| "Check My Rate" button that performs soft credit pull | CRITICAL | Requires broker licensing in most states |
| Collecting SSN, DOB, or full name for matching | CRITICAL | Lead generation / broker activity |
| Showing personalized APR rates based on user's credit | CRITICAL | Requires broker licensing |
| "Pre-qualified" or "Pre-approved" labels | HIGH | FTC fined Credit Karma $3M for this |
| Collecting income/employment data | HIGH | Moves from editorial to lead gen |
| Passing user financial data to lenders | HIGH | Broker/lead gen activity, requires licensing |
| "Apply Now" button that submits data to lender on CreditDoc's behalf | HIGH | Acting as intermediary |
| Specific monthly payment amounts without full Reg Z disclosures | MEDIUM | Triggers Regulation Z requirements |
| Calculator that says "You'd pay $X/month" for a specific lender | MEDIUM | Could be seen as term negotiation |
| Ranking lenders higher because they pay more without disclosure | CRITICAL | FTC deceptive practices -- LendEDU was fined $350K |
| Using "Recommended" or "Best Match" labels influenced by compensation | HIGH | CFPB Circular 2024-01 abusive practices |

### Specific APR Guidance

**SAFE to show:**
- Lender's published APR range (e.g., "SoFi: 8.99% - 25.49% APR") -- this is publicly available information
- Average industry APR data (e.g., "Average personal loan APR: 12.35%") -- editorial/statistical content
- APR range disclaimer: "Actual rates depend on creditworthiness, loan amount, and term"

**AVOID:**
- Specific monthly payment amounts tied to APR (triggers Reg Z)
- "You could get X% APR" (implies personalization without data)
- Advertising a specific rate as "available to you" without proper licensing

### Pre-Qualification

**CreditDoc should AVOID pre-qualification entirely** in its initial launch. The reasons:
- Requires multi-state broker licensing (expensive, complex)
- Requires NMLS registration
- Requires compliance infrastructure (call center, dispute resolution, etc.)
- Credit Karma's $3M FTC fine shows the risk of getting this wrong
- CreditDoc's strength is editorial quality, not marketplace functionality

### Credit Score / Income Collection

**CreditDoc should NOT ask for:**
- Exact credit score (user can self-select a range)
- Income amount
- SSN
- Date of birth
- Full legal name
- Address (beyond state for lender availability filtering)

**CreditDoc CAN ask for (as editorial filters):**
- Credit score RANGE (Excellent/Good/Fair/Poor)
- Loan amount RANGE
- Loan purpose
- State of residence

## 6. AFFILIATE CONSIDERATIONS

### How Personal Loan Affiliate Programs Work

**Commission structures:**

| Type | How It Works | Typical Payout | Risk Level |
|------|-------------|----------------|------------|
| Per Click (CPC) | Paid when user clicks affiliate link | $0.50 - $5.00 | Lowest risk |
| Per Lead (CPL) | Paid when user submits application on lender's site | $20 - $300 | Medium risk |
| Per Funded Loan (CPA) | Paid when loan is funded | $60 - $500+ | Medium risk |
| Revenue Share | Percentage of lender's revenue | Up to 76% | Higher risk |

**Specific lender programs (researched):**
- SoFi: $100-$150 per lead, $300+ per funded personal loan
- LightStream: $60 per funded loan
- Upstart: $120 per funded loan
- PersonalLoans.com: 76% revenue share per approved loan
- Network aggregators (Round Sky, LeadsMarket, Lead Stack Media): $150-$300 per lead

### Does Accepting Affiliate Commissions Create Regulatory Obligations?

**For CreditDoc's editorial model (click-through affiliate links): LOW RISK**

If CreditDoc operates like Bankrate:
- User clicks "Visit Site" or "Learn More" link
- Link goes to lender's own website (with affiliate tracking)
- User applies directly on lender's site
- CreditDoc earns commission from the click or resulting application

This is standard affiliate marketing and does NOT create broker obligations because:
- CreditDoc does not collect user financial data
- CreditDoc does not perform credit checks
- CreditDoc does not negotiate terms
- The user is redirected to the lender's own application process
- CreditDoc is a publisher, not an intermediary

**HOWEVER, CreditDoc MUST:**
1. Disclose affiliate relationships on every page with lender listings
2. Not let affiliate commissions influence editorial ratings
3. Not rank lenders higher because they pay higher commissions (unless clearly labeled as "Sponsored" or "Ad")
4. Include clear "Advertiser Disclosure" language

### How Major Sites Disclose Affiliate Relationships

**NerdWallet's approach (recommended model for CreditDoc):**
- "Advertiser Disclosure" label at top of comparison pages
- Full disclosure page explaining how they make money
- Statement: "Partner compensation is one of several factors that may affect which products are highlighted"
- But: "These factors do not influence editors' opinions or ratings"
- Separate licenses/disclosures page for legal compliance

**Bankrate's approach:**
- "We are an independent, advertising-supported comparison service"
- "We are compensated in exchange for placement of sponsored products"
- Firewall between advertisers and editorial team

## 7. RECOMMENDATIONS FOR CREDITDOC

### Operating Model: "Bankrate Model" (Editorial Publisher/Directory)

## APPENDIX: KEY SOURCES AND ENFORCEMENT ACTIONS

### FTC Enforcement Actions Against Financial Comparison Sites

1. **LendEDU (2020):** $350,000 fine for deceptive rankings and fake reviews. Rankings were pay-for-play but claimed to be "objective."
2. **Credit Karma (2022):** $3 million fine for deceptive "pre-approved" claims. Nearly 1/3 of "pre-approved" users were rejected.
3. **Response Tree (2024):** $7 million fine for illegal lead generation practices.

### Key Regulations

- **Truth in Lending Act (TILA) / Regulation Z:** 12 CFR 1026
- **CFPB Circular 2024-01:** Preferencing and steering by digital intermediaries (may be rescinded under current administration)
- **FTC Endorsement Guides:** 16 CFR Part 255
- **California Financing Law (CFL):** Administered by DFPI
- **SAFE Act:** Mortgage loan originator licensing (applies to personal loans via lender licensing)
